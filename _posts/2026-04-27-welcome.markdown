I do offensive security work for a living. Web apps, APIs, cloud, internal networks, and the identity systems that hold all of it together. After about five years of writing client reports that almost nobody outside the engagement reads, I want a place to write things down where I can find them again, and where someone else might find them useful too.

This site is that place.

## What you'll find here

**Writeups.** When I find a bug worth explaining, I'll walk through it: how I found it, what made it exploitable, what the impact actually was, and how I'd fix it. Everything here is reproduced in a lab or pulled from public material. Nothing client-specific, ever.

**Technique posts.** Short pieces on a single attack chain or pattern. Kerberoasting, AD CS misconfigurations, SSRF gadgets, IAM trust-policy abuse, that kind of thing. Less narrative, more "here's the payload, here's why it works."

**Tooling.** Small Python and Bash scripts I keep reaching for. Rough edges left in.

**Reading notes.** CVE deep-dives, papers, threat actor TTPs I want to remember.

## A note on format

Every writeup here aims to be reproducible. That means a lab-setup section, the exact commands and payloads, the failure modes I hit on the way to the working version, and a remediation block at the end. If a post can't be reproduced from what's on the page, it's not a writeup, it's a tweet.

## A note on what's *not* here

Nothing represents the views of any employer, past or present. No client names, no sanitized internal hostnames, no screenshots with identifying bytes left in them. If something isn't in a public CVE, a public bug bounty disclosure, or a lab I built myself, I'm not writing about it.

Thanks for reading.
